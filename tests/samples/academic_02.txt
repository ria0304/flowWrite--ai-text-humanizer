Contemporary climate science has established with high confidence that anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions are the primary driver of observed global temperature increases since the mid-twentieth century. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has synthesized extensive empirical data demonstrating that surface temperatures have risen approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial baselines, with projections indicating further warming trajectories contingent upon future emissions scenarios.

The multifaceted consequences of climate change encompass a broad spectrum of environmental and socioeconomic impacts. Rising sea levels, driven by thermal expansion of ocean waters and accelerated glacial melt, pose existential threats to low-lying coastal communities and island nations. Furthermore, shifts in precipitation patterns are projected to exacerbate water scarcity in already vulnerable regions, while simultaneously increasing flood risks in others. The compounding nature of these impacts necessitates urgent and coordinated international policy responses.

Mitigation strategies must encompass both technological innovation and fundamental transformations in energy systems, land use practices, and consumption patterns. Carbon capture and storage technologies, renewable energy deployment, and nature-based solutions represent promising avenues for emissions reduction. However, it is imperative to acknowledge that adaptation measures will also be required to address unavoidable climate impacts already locked into the Earth system due to historical emissions.
